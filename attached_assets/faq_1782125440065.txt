How to Choose a Web Agency: 7 Questions You Should Ask

Choosing a web agency is a business decision. It requires time, research, and a clear understanding of what you need.

Many businesses focus only on price and forget about long-term value. A website should support your business, not become another problem.

Here are 7 questions that help separate professional agencies from companies that simply deliver a template and move on.

1. Do you have experience in my industry?

An agency that works with every type of business may not always have deep experience in a specific area.

Ask what projects they have completed in your industry and what results they achieved.

Look at more than just design. Consider performance, usability, conversions, and business impact.

2. What is your discovery process?

A professional agency should not start building after a short conversation.

The first stage should include understanding your goals, customers, competitors, and current challenges.

If the process starts with choosing a template, important details may be missed.

3. Do you focus on SEO and performance?

A website needs to do more than look good.

It should load quickly, work well across devices, and help customers find your business.

Ask how they approach technical SEO, speed, and measuring results.

4. Who will manage my project?

Clear communication matters.

You should know who is responsible for your project and who understands your business goals.

A dedicated contact person keeps the process organised.

5. What happens after launch?

A website needs ongoing care.

Ask about updates, security, backups, technical support, and future improvements.

A launch should be the beginning, not the end.

6. Can I move my website if my needs change?

Your website should belong to you.

A good agency builds with flexibility in mind, allowing you to change providers without losing control of your data or domain.

7. How is pricing structured?

Pricing should be clear from the beginning.

You should understand what is included, what happens if the project changes, and what future costs may appear.

Summary

Choose an agency that takes time to understand your business.

The right questions should come from both sides. A good agency wants to understand your goals before building anything.


Responsiveness vs Speed: Why Both Matter for Conversions

Most people browse the internet using mobile devices.

But responsiveness and speed are two different things.

A website needs to look good on every screen and load quickly. Both directly affect user experience and conversions.

What is responsiveness?

A responsive website automatically adapts to different screen sizes.

Text, images, buttons, and navigation adjust to work properly on phones, tablets, and desktops.

This is no longer an extra feature. It is a basic requirement for modern websites.

What is loading speed?

Speed is how quickly a website loads and becomes usable.

A slow website creates frustration. Visitors leave before they contact you, make a purchase, or explore your services.

Speed also affects SEO because search engines consider performance when evaluating websites.

How do they work together?

A website can be responsive but poorly optimised.

It may look good on mobile but load slowly because of unnecessary code or large files.

A fast website that is not mobile-friendly creates another problem. Visitors struggle with navigation and leave.

Both areas need attention.

How do you measure speed?

LCP (Largest Contentful Paint):
Measures how quickly the main content loads.

FID (First Input Delay):
Measures how quickly the website responds to interaction.

CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift):
Measures whether the page moves unexpectedly while loading.

These metrics show how users experience your website.

How do you build a fast and responsive website?

Efficient code:
Avoid unnecessary scripts and keep the website lightweight.

Optimised images:
Use modern formats, compression, and lazy loading.

Smart structure:
Use caching and performance improvements where needed.

Real testing:
Check the website on real devices and different connections.

Summary

Responsiveness helps people use your website.

Speed helps people stay on your website.

Both are essential for conversions.


Why a CMS Template Is Not the Same as a Custom Website

A template CMS can be a quick and affordable solution.

However, as a business grows, limitations can appear.

Templates are designed for many users. Custom websites are designed around your specific goals.

Advantages of a template CMS

Lower initial cost.

Faster setup.

Simple editing for non-technical users.

Many features available through existing tools.

Limitations of a template CMS

Many websites look similar.

Custom changes can become difficult.

Too many plugins can affect performance and security.

Moving to another system can become complicated.

Advantages of a custom website

Unique design and functionality.

Better performance through cleaner code.

Full control over SEO, integrations, and data.

Ability to grow with your business.

Easier long-term flexibility.

Limitations of a custom website

Higher initial investment.

Requires proper development and maintenance.

Who should use a template?

Templates work well for small projects, simple websites, portfolios, or businesses testing an idea.

Who should use a custom website?

Custom development makes more sense for businesses focused on growth, strong user experience, integrations, and long-term flexibility.

Summary

A template is a quick solution.

A custom website is built around your business.


E-commerce: Why Speed and User Experience Drive Sales

In online retail, small problems can create lost sales.

Speed, trust, and a simple buying process all influence whether customers complete a purchase.

1. Speed affects conversions

Customers expect online shops to respond quickly.

A slow website creates hesitation and increases abandoned visits.

Every second matters when someone is ready to buy.

2. Checkout experience

The buying process should be simple.

Too many steps, hidden costs, or limited payment options create unnecessary barriers.

A smooth checkout helps customers complete their purchase.

3. Trust signals

Customers need confidence before sharing payment details.

Clear information, secure connections, reviews, policies, and contact details all help build trust.

A professional website removes doubts.

4. Mobile shopping

Many customers browse and buy using phones.

Mobile shops need:

Easy navigation.

Clear buttons.

Fast-loading images.

Simple product search.

The experience should feel natural on a smaller screen.

5. Inventory and product management

Customers expect accurate information.

Stock levels, product updates, and recommendations should work correctly.

Small details influence buying decisions.

Summary

E-commerce success depends on removing friction.

Every part of the customer journey affects sales.


SEO: Why Your Website Is Only the Beginning

Building a website does not automatically bring customers from Google.

SEO combines technical quality, content, user experience, and online authority.

Your website is the foundation, not the entire strategy.

Website foundation

Structure:

A clear website structure helps search engines understand your pages.

Speed:

Performance affects both user experience and visibility.

Mobile experience:

Search engines focus heavily on mobile versions of websites.

Technical setup:

Correct indexing, structured data, and clean code help search engines read your content.

Content matters

Good content answers real customer questions.

It should match what people are searching for and provide useful information.

Quality matters more than adding unnecessary words.

Experience and trust

Search engines look for signs that a business is reliable.

Clear information, expertise, customer feedback, and real experience help build authority.

Links and mentions

Other websites linking to your business can strengthen your reputation.

Quality matters more than quantity.

User behaviour

How people interact with your website gives search engines signals.

If visitors quickly leave or cannot find what they need, it can affect performance.

Technical SEO basics

Robots.txt:
Controls which pages search engines should access.

Sitemap:
Helps search engines discover your content.

Redirects:
Protect rankings when pages change.

Summary

SEO is ongoing work.

A website creates the foundation.

Content, optimisation, and trust build long-term visibility.
